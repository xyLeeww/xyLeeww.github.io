## Graphics Pipeline

### What is Graphics Pipeline?

Graphics pipeline is a series of procedures for transforming a 3D scene into a 2D representation on a screen. Graphics application programming interfaces (APIs), such as Direct3D, OpenGL and Vulkan were developed to standardize common procedures and oversee the graphics pipeline of a given hardware accelerator.

The model of the graphics pipeline is usually used in real-time rendering. Often, most of the pipeline steps are implemented in hardware, which allows for special optimizations.

### Shader Stages

![shader stages](/images/vulkan_simplified_pipeline.svg)
Stages with a green color are known as fixed-function stages. These stages allow you to tweak their operations using parameters, but the way they work is predefined.

Stages with an orange color on the other hand are programmable, which means that you can upload your own code to the graphics card to apply exactly the operations you want.
Some fixed-function can now be implemented using compute shaders, such as software rasterization、custom blending.

#### Vertex Shader

On modern hardware, most of the geometry computation steps are performed in the vertex shader. This is, in principle, freely programmable, but generally performs at least the transformation of the points and the illumination calculation.

Vertex transformation can be broken down into 5 steps:

- Model Matrix: Transforms local object coordinates into world coordinates.
- View Matrix: Transforms world coordinates into camera-relative coordinates.
- Projection Matrix: Maps the view frustum into a normalized clip volume.
- NDC: Normalized device coords.
- Viewport Transform: From clip space to final pixel coordinates on screen.

The complete MVP transformation:

$$
\vec{v}_{clip} = M_{projection} \cdot M_{view} \cdot M_{model} \cdot \vec{v}_{local}
$$

$$
\vec{v}_{clip} = (x_{clip}, y_{clip}, z_{clip}, w_{clip})^T
$$

Where each matrix is a $4 \times 4$ transformation:

$$
M_{model} = \begin{bmatrix} R & T \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

- $R$ — $3 \times 3$ rotation matrix (object orientation in world space)
- $T$ — $3 \times 1$ translation vector (object position in world space)

$$
M_{view} = \begin{bmatrix} r_x & r_y & r_z & -\vec{r} \cdot \vec{eye} \\ u_x & u_y & u_z & -\vec{u} \cdot \vec{eye} \\ f_x & f_y & f_z & -\vec{f} \cdot \vec{eye} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

- $\vec{r}$ — camera right axis
- $\vec{u}$ — camera up axis
- $\vec{f}$ — camera forward axis (gaze direction)
- $\vec{eye}$ — camera position in world space

$$
M_{projection} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{2n}{r-l} & 0 & \frac{r+l}{r-l} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{2n}{t-b} & \frac{t+b}{t-b} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\frac{f+n}{f-n} & -\frac{2fn}{f-n} \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

- $n, f$ — near and far clipping plane distances
- $l, r$ — left and right bounds of the view frustum at the near plane
- $t, b$ — top and bottom bounds of the view frustum at the near plane

The shader code for this part can be found at [shader.vert](https://github.com/xyLeeww/HelloTriangle/blob/main/shaders/shader.vert).

#### Tessellation

Tessellation is a pipeline stage that subdivides low-polygon patches into more detailed geometry at runtime.

#### Geometry Shader

Geometry Shader is an optional programmable stage that receives complete primitives (points, lines, or triangles) as input and can output zero or more new primitives. On modern GPUs, geometry shaders have poor parallelism due to variable output size and serial execution constraints. They have been largely superseded by mesh shaders (Vulkan/DX12) and compute shader-based approaches.

Capabilities:

- Amplification
- Culling
- Layered rendering

#### Rasterization

In the rasterization step, discrete fragments are created from continuous primitives. Each fragment corresponds to one pixel in the frame buffer and this corresponds to one pixel of the screen.

#### Fragment Shader

Fragment shader (also called pixel shader) is a programmable stage that computes the final color and other attributes for each fragment generated by rasterization. Each fragment is processed independently, making fragment shaders highly parallelizable on GPU hardware. However, they execute per-pixel, so complex shaders on high-resolution screens are a common performance bottleneck (fill-rate bound).

Capabilities:

- Texture sampling
- Lighting calculation
- Normal mapping
- Shadow mapping
- Discard

#### Stencil Test

Stencil test is a fixed-function per-fragment operation that whether a fragment is visibal by comparing its stencil value against reference. In Vulkan, depth and stencil are configured in `VkPipelineDepthStencilStateCreateInfo`.

#### Depth Test

Depth test (also known as Z-test) is a per-fragment operation that determines whether a fragment is visible by comparing its depth value against the value already stored in the depth buffer (Z-buffer). It is the primary mechanism for hidden surface removal, ensuring that closer objects occlude farther ones.
Logically, depth test occurs after the fragment shader and before color blending. However, modern GPUs perform Early-Z testing before the fragment shader as an optimization when possible.

Early-Z: Depth test executes before the fragment shader. The GPU compares depth first — if the fragment fails, it's discarded immediately, skipping all shading work. Early-Z is a hardware optimization that skips shading for occluded fragments entirely. To maximize Early-Z efficiency, render opaque objects front-to-back。
Late-Z: Depth test executes after the fragment shader. The fragment goes through full shading first, then depth comparison decides whether to write.

The GPU automatically falls back to late-Z when:

- Shader writes gl_FragDepth / SV_Depth
- Uses discard / clip()
- Alpha-to-coverage is enabled
- Certain special combinations e.g. depth write off + stencil write on

#### Color Belending

Color blending is a fixed-function stage that combines the fragment shader's output color with the color already stored in the framebuffer at that pixel location. This is the final step before a pixel value is written to the framebuffer. Without blending, each new fragment simply overwrites the framebuffer — fine for opaque objects. But for transparency, particles, UI overlays, etc., we need to combine the new color with what's already there.
